OPINION | Why the First-Instance Verdict Against Yoon Suk Yeol is Legally Flawed

Jean Cummings
Political Columnist / Former Publisher, The Asia Post
February 19, 2026

"Is South Korea's insurrection verdict a legal judgment or political condemnation?" My latest analysis on why the 1st-instance verdict against Yoon Suk Yeol fails the test of modern jurisprudence and strict legal interpretation.

Overextension of the Legal Concept of 'Riot'

What I find difficult to accept in today’s first-instance verdict is the legal application of the concept of a "riot." Regarding the definition of a riot under Article 91 of the Criminal Act, the Supreme Court has consistently ruled it to be an "exercise of power accompanied by the highest level of violence or intimidation."

However, during the emergency martial law in question, no live ammunition was used, there was almost no active exercise of physical force, and no casualties occurred. No direct violence by military troops was confirmed, and the functions of the National Assembly were, in fact, continuously exercised.

Despite this, the court of first instance judged the armed entry under the President's emergency martial law as a riot. I view this as an excessive expansion and interpretation of the concept of a riot. Penal statutes must be interpreted strictly, and under the principle of nullum crimen sine lege (no crime without law), the constituent elements of a crime must not be expanded through interpretation.

Lack of Strict Proof Regarding the Intent to Subvert the Constitution

The crime of insurrection does not exist simply by the act of declaring martial law; it is a "specific intent crime" where the "purpose of subverting the constitutional order" must be strictly proven. This is predicated on the grand principle of criminal trials: "Proof beyond a reasonable doubt." However, the first-instance court recognized the crime of insurrection based on individual illegal acts, such as the attempted arrest of politicians.

This is a matter that should be handled as separate crimes, such as "abuse of authority," and concluding that these acts constitute a purpose to overthrow the state is a clear logical leap. No direct evidence was presented to show that the attempt to isolate specific politicians constituted a definitive intent to destroy the entire constitutional order.

In particular, the court presumed a purpose of "permanent paralysis" because the end time of the martial law was not specified. Legally, "indefinite" cannot equate to "permanent." This violates the principle of prohibiting expanded interpretation to the detriment of the defendant. Not specifying a time in law simply means the end point is fluid. Interpreting this as an attempt to "permanently paralyze constitutional institutions" is a logical leap that distorts facts in the direction most unfavorable to the defendant.

On the contrary, the factual evidence—that the President immediately complied with the National Assembly's demand for rescission and withdrew the troops—is powerful circumstantial evidence that there was no intent to subvert the constitution. For the first instance to exclude this substantive behavioral evidence and rely on "ex-post reasoning" constitutes a violation of the principle of evidence-based trials. Recognizing intent based on mere estimation does not align with the principle of the burden of proof in a major criminal trial. In the appellate stage, the prosecution's burden of proof regarding whether the defendants had a definitive intent to overthrow the constitutional order must be scrutinized more strictly.

Legal Issues in the Establishment of Insurrection

It is difficult to view the crime of insurrection as being established by a mere commencement of execution; it requires a substantive force capable of rendering the functions of state institutions virtually impossible. 
However, the functions of the National Assembly were restored within a short period, the resolution to lift martial law was possible, and the President immediately complied with the demand for rescission. In other words, the constitutional order was swiftly restored.

Even if illegality is recognized, this may be evaluated as an attempted crime or abuse of authority, but judging it as a completed crime of insurrection clearly requires a legal re-examination.

The Boundary Between Constitutional Article 77 and Criminal Responsibility

Article 77 of the Constitution grants the President the authority to declare martial law. 
Martial law, by its nature, involves the mobilization of troops, restrictions on movement, and the strengthening of public power.  If this is immediately evaluated as a criminal riot, the power to declare martial law becomes a power that inherently carries the risk of criminal punishment.

If the "attempted entry of armed soldiers" itself is viewed as a riot, there is a risk that the constitutional authority of any future president to declare martial law could be completely neutralized by the fear of criminal prosecution.  If so, the constitutional emergency power system is in danger of being reduced to a practical state of abolition. While the abuse of martial law power requires judicial control, expanding that boundary immediately to the crime of insurrection under criminal law must be handled with extreme caution.

The Issue of Proportionality in Sentencing

Compared to the past trials of the 12/12 and 5/18 incidents, this situation involved zero loss of life and lasted for an extremely short duration. Even if illegality is recognized, considering that there were no casualties, the incident ended quickly, the demand for rescission was immediately accepted, and the constitutional order was restored, it is questionable whether a sentence of life imprisonment complies with the principle of proportionality between responsibility and punishment.

Even if the law stipulates that "a leader shall be sentenced to death or life imprisonment," the question of whether life imprisonment conforms to the principle of responsibility—considering the specific danger of the act and the actual results—must be re-examined in the second instance.

Anachronistic Legal Logic and Lack of Modern Reality

The most bewildering point of this verdict is that the court, rather than citing substantive examples from modern Western democratic states, summoned "relics from the museum" such as the era of Roman Emperors, medieval monarchies, and the 17th-century case of King Charles I of England.

While it is common for a trial to cite past precedents, summoning a 17th-century absolute monarch or Charles I while judging the actions of a 21st-century president of a democratic nation is an excessive logical leap. Today's politics operate within highly institutionalized safeguards, mature civic consciousness, and complex power structures.

By applying the framework of throne usurpation from ancient and medieval times to the actions of the President of the Republic of Korea in the 21st century, 
the verdict completely overlooked the advanced political structure and cultural context of modern democracy. The court even mentioned cases of developing nations while ignoring the modern governance examples of advanced nations with which Korea is closely aligned.

Law should be the minimum of common sense. Is it sensible to judge that the citizens living in 2026 can be persuaded by the logic of the Roman era? 
A criminal trial is not archaeology interpreting ancient artifacts. it must reflect the universal values shared by contemporaries and the actual realities of governance.

It is an extreme and dangerous judgment to trap a desperate governmental decision in the radical frame of "insurrection" under the vague premise that such things do not happen in advanced nations due to institutional safeguards. I cannot help but ask if this verdict aligns with the "rationality" of modern jurisprudence.

Urging the Establishment of the Rule of Law

I believe this first-instance verdict was overly consequentialist in its legal application and failed to secure judicial fairness by applying an excessively broad interpretation of the constituent elements of the crime.

First, the unreasonable expansion of the concept of "riot." In a situation where there was no substantive physical destruction, no violence by the military, and zero casualties, defining the mere armed entry itself as a riot of insurrection is an interpretation that deviates from the principle of nullum crimen sine lege.

Second, the lack of proof regarding the "purpose of subverting the constitution." Despite insurrection being a specific intent crime requiring strict proof, the court recognized intent based on ex-post reasoning rather than the objective fact that the President immediately accepted the demand to lift martial law and swiftly withdrew the troops.

Third, the violation of the principle of proportionality in sentencing. From the legal perspective of Western democratic nations, including the U.S., sentencing a defendant to the maximum penalty of life imprisonment for an incident that did not involve loss of life or permanent paralysis of state functions will likely face criticism for being a "political condemnation" rather than a "legal judgment."

Furthermore, before a judgment on the merits, a significant procedural dispute remains regarding the legitimacy of the investigative power. Whether the crime of insurrection is explicitly included in the scope of investigation under the Prosecution Service Act for High-Ranking Officials, and its relationship with the President's privilege of immunity under Article 84 of the Constitution, requires stricter legal review in the appellate court. The fact that a heavy sentence was handed down before these procedural regularities and the scope of authority were clarified must be re-examined for the sake of judicial justice.

The question of whether insurrection was established will now be fully contested in the second instance. I hope that the appellate court will conduct a cool-headed and fair legal re-examination, free from emotion and political tides. By thoroughly discerning the truth of evidence and testimony, I look forward to a righteous verdict that all citizens, regardless of their political affiliation, can rationally accept. That is the only way to restore the shaken rule of law in South Korea and protect the values of democracy.
